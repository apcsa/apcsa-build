Behaviors in Java are called methods. Each method has a heading that gives its name, the type of information it provides, and the names and types of its parameters. It also has a body, a sequence of steps—statements—that it performs to produce its result.

Steps in a method body are similar to steps in a recipe. For example, the method representing the behavior of a calculator's accumulator behavior might involve the following steps:

  1. Convert a sequence of digit keystrokes into a number
  2. Decide whether to add or subtract that number to the value already accumulated, or replace that value
  3. Display the result

This is not so different from:

  1. Put a cup of blueberries into a blender.
  2. Put a cup of vanilla ice cream into the blender.
  3. Put a cup of milk into the blender.
  4. Blend.

What's the outcome? This method that produces a blueberry milk shake.

Translating a model to a program will involve extra behaviors that represent ways to access state information. For example, a shoe in the real world doesn't tell us its size color, or style; we just see that when we look at the shoe. In a computer model, however, we need behaviors to access this information because we can't "see" it otherwise.
